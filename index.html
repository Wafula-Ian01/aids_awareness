Key Points

HIV is a virus that attacks the body's immune system.

The only way to know if you have HIV is to get tested.

There are many ways to prevent HIV — PrEP, PEP, condoms, and not sharing needles.

HIV treatment helps people live long, healthy lives and prevents transmission.

Overview

HIV (human immunodeficiency virus) is a virus that attacks the body's immune system. Without treatment, it can lead to AIDS (acquired immunodeficiency syndrome).

There is currently no effective cure. Once people get HIV, they have it for life. But proper medical care can control the virus. People with HIV who get on and stay on effective HIV treatment can live long, healthy lives and protect their partners.

Symptoms

Most people have flu-like symptoms within 2 to 4 weeks after infection. Symptoms may last for a few days or several weeks.

Fever

A sudden high body temperature is often one of the first signs of acute HIV infection, usually appearing within 2–4 weeks of exposure. It is often accompanied by night sweats and chills.

Acute Stage Symptom
Sore Throat

Pain or irritation in the throat is a common early symptom that resembles a typical cold or flu. It may make swallowing uncomfortable and can persist for several days or weeks.

Acute Stage Symptom
Swollen Lymph Nodes

The immune system responds to infection by enlarging lymph nodes, particularly in the neck, armpits, and groin. These swellings can be tender to the touch and may last for weeks.

Acute Stage Symptom
Rash

A red, flat or slightly raised rash may appear on the trunk, face, or limbs — often described as blotchy or spotty. This skin reaction is the body's inflammatory response to the new infection.

Acute Stage Symptom
Muscle Aches

Generalised muscle and joint pain — myalgia — makes everyday movement uncomfortable. Similar to body aches felt during the flu, this happens as the immune system mounts its initial response to the virus.

Acute Stage Symptom
Fatigue

Extreme tiredness and a lack of energy that rest doesn't relieve is one of the most common early symptoms. The body exhausts its resources fighting the virus, leaving a person feeling drained and unable to concentrate.

Acute Stage Symptom

Having these symptoms alone doesn't mean you have HIV — other illnesses can cause similar symptoms. Some people have no symptoms at all. The only way to know if you have HIV is to get tested.

How It Spreads

Most people who get HIV get it through anal or vaginal sex, or sharing needles, syringes, or other drug injection equipment. Only certain body fluids can transmit HIV:

Blood
Semen (cum)
Pre-seminal fluid
Rectal fluids
Vaginal fluids
Breast milk

These fluids must come in contact with a mucous membrane or damaged tissue, or be directly injected into the bloodstream for transmission to occur.

Factors like a person's viral load, other sexually transmitted infections, and alcohol or drug use can increase the chances of transmitting HIV.

Prevention

Today, more tools than ever are available to prevent HIV. Prevention strategies include:

Condoms
Use condoms the right way every time you have sex.
PrEP
Pre-exposure prophylaxis — taken before potential HIV exposure.
PEP
Post-exposure prophylaxis — taken after a potential HIV exposure.
No Needle Sharing
Never share needles, syringes, or other drug injection equipment.

If you have HIV, taking HIV treatment to get and keep an undetectable viral load is one of the most powerful ways to protect your partners.

Testing

The only way to know your HIV status is to get tested. Knowing your status gives you powerful information to keep yourself and others healthy.

Get tested for HIV

Everyone between the ages of 13 and 64 should get tested at least once. People with certain risk factors should get tested more often. Testing options are quick, free, and painless.

If your result is positive, you can take medicine to treat HIV and live a long, healthy life while protecting others. If your result is negative, you can take actions to prevent HIV.

Treatment

HIV treatment (antiretroviral therapy or ART) involves taking medicine as prescribed by a health care provider. You should start treatment as soon as possible after diagnosis.

HIV treatment can make the viral load so low that a test can't detect it (undetectable viral load). If you have an undetectable viral load, you will not transmit HIV to others through sex.

How HIV Progresses

When people with HIV don't get treatment, they typically progress through three stages. HIV treatment can slow or prevent the disease from progressing. With modern treatment, progression to Stage 3 (AIDS) is less common today.

1
Acute HIV Infection
  • Large amount of HIV in blood — very contagious
  • Many people experience flu-like symptoms
  • Get tested if you suspect exposure
2
Chronic HIV Infection (Clinical Latency)
  • HIV is still active and reproducing in the body
  • People may have no symptoms but can still transmit HIV
  • May last a decade or longer without treatment
  • People on treatment may never progress beyond this stage
3
AIDS — Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome
  • CD4 cell count drops below 200 cells/mL of blood
  • High viral load — easily transmitted to others
  • Severely damaged immune system
  • Without treatment, survival is typically about 3 years
